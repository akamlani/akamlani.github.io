NuPIC

NuPIC is an open source community project focusing on a set of learning algorithms of how layers of neurons in the neocortex learn, centered around the Hierarchical Temporal Memory (HTM) model. It's implementation is most closely modeled after layer 3 in the Neocortex. Reference the cortical learning algorithm (CLA) and NuPIC github repositories for more information.

My interest followed based on the release of the book "On Intelligence" by Jeff Hawkins initially released in 2005. Since then the research and product offering has evolved. The key differentiators are: Sparse Distributed Representations (SDRs), Temporal Inference, and Online Learning.

This past November, I attended the Fall Hackathon 2013. This was the first real introduction to NuPIC for the general public in the form of a Hackathon. It consisted of a mix of informational sessions, presentations and presented hacks using the framework. As quoted from the mailing list:

I think we had a nice mix of people at the event, e.g. from those focused on neuroscience biological aspects, to those just getting their feet wet, a few hackers applying NuPIC to some applications, and the great staff. Great new introductions, good conversations and informational sessions. Definitely an event worth attending!
- Ari Kamlani
NuPIC Hackathon

My hackathon activities consisted of two different projects. First I ported NuPIC over to a Chromebook running Ubuntu on ARM architecture. Ideally this would be ported to some of the current devices running latter versions of ARM Architecture (ARMv7, Cortex A15). The Chromebook allowed native compilation without having to cross compile and deal with external storage or flashing.

Second, I used the temporal nature of RSSI fluctuation for localization, to perform Wi-Fi Sample Prediction 'x' samples into the future. The prediction measurements were performed by walking a repeated pattern that would generalize over time. The demo was performed using Wi-Fi scanning from a laptop based on the advertised SSID and statistics in Wi-Fi Direct mode, taking into account real world interference and physical barrier problems. It would be interesting to see how this implementation compares against a more accurate time pulse based solution not using RSSI (UWB, IEEE 802.15.4 with ranging methods).

UHF RFID, Energy Harvesting

Classic Passive UHF RFID ICs are still very limited in terms of range. The BAP and Active model are able to increase the range, but at a higher BOM. Part of research at Nagra is developing a POC is over the Passive market limitations and increase the range below the cost of even a traditional BAP, within FCC limits. This involves aspsects such as increasing the sensitivity, altering encoding, amplification, and antenna design. The goal is to then use this design in a disposable form factor for localization.

In order to power some of the circuits requiring external power for this RFID design, my research has also involved energy harvesting. Current focus is on the 2.4 GHz band for some of the ambient Wi-Fi and BT signals. The difficult part is in overcoming the passive thresholds of the rectifiers and minimizing DC/DC boost converter input voltage and start-up voltages. Simulations on Agilent ADS have been focused with an input @-30 dBm at the antenna.

UWB

Instead of performing ranging and localization based on RSSI narrowband solutions, a Time Based Pulse Oriented (TOF, TOA) Wideband for better accuracy could be obtained. More expensive coherent solutions can increase the range, closer to Active RFID distances. Part of my work involves technology assessment, presentations, and vendor engagement.

WiMAX, LTE

Earlier WiMAX based solutions were based on a RNDIS and Hostless PC Dongle, in contrast to the embedded solution I worked on. Much of my focus was on interopability and increasing performance throughput.

Prototyping Platforms
Electric Imp

Electric Imp is emplowering the Internet of Things (IoT), connecting the physical world (Device) with the services world (Agent, Server). The user code is written in a minimal form of the Squirrel Language and debugged in their cloud based IDE. The device has an integrated ARM Cortex M3 Processor and WiFi in the form of SD Card w/Socket Module (Imp001) vs Soldered Down Model (Imp002). The WiFi configuration consists of a unique Proprietary solution for device setup, via a Blinkup Method. Reference some of their services and examples via their Github Repo.

TI BeagleBone, BeagleBoard

The TI BeagleBone Black edition is the newest model from TI. It no longer runs the TI OMAP Processor, like the Beagle Boards. I had previously worked with several OMAP flavours (353x, 443x) on SoC platforms. The "Black" version removed several onboard components in order to compete with the Raspberry Pi, e.g. USB JTAG, USB to Serial converter, etc. The development platform is based on the TI AM335x 1GHz ARM Cortex-A8 Processor and comes with Angstrom. However I prefer using the Ubuntu console image with a minimal Root FS (RFS).

Gumstix

Gumstix provides a Computer on Modules (COM) form factor. The model which I have worked on is the Overo Fire. It is based on a TI OMAP 3530 (ARM Cortex-A8) Application Processor, similar to the BeagleBoard (not BeagleBone) earlier processors. It was used on the Nascar Truck Series project for the Application Processor running a minimal RFS from scratch. Reference my Github Repo for further details.
